Inclusive language aims to remove barriers in communication, ensuring that all users can understand and interact with the product effectively, irrespective of their background, gender, culture, or abilities. It's not just a best practiceâ€”it's a necessity in our increasingly diverse society.

Building an accessible and equitable digital experience extends beyond coding practices and visual design. The words we use in the UI significantly affect the experience for all users, including those who use assistive technologies.

<Heading name="Principles of Inclusive Language
" level="h2" style="h2" type="article" />
  <div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-8">
  <div>
    <Image alt="Example image" src="https://placehold.co/500x250"/>
    <Heading name="Clarity" level="h3" style="4" type="article" />
    Opt for simple, straightforward words and phrases. Language should be easy to understand to make the application accessible to users who speak English as an additional language, or who have cognitive disabilities.
  </div>

{' '}

<div>
  <Image alt="Example image" src="https://placehold.co/500x250" />
  <Heading
    name="Consistency
  "
    level="h3"
    style="4"
    type="article"
  />
  Use terminology consistently across your app. This consistency aids in
  learning and removes confusion, especially for screen reader users.
</div>

<div>
  <Image alt="Example image" src="https://placehold.co/500x250" />
  <Heading
    name="Sensitivity
  "
    level="h3"
    style="4"
    type="article"
  />
  Avoid terms that can marginalize or stigmatize groups of people based on
  aspects like gender, race, or ability. This also includes avoiding idioms that
  may not translate well.
</div>

<div>
    <Image alt="Example image" src="https://placehold.co/500x250"/>
  <Heading name="Affirmation" level="h3" style="4" type="article" />
  Choose words that respect and affirm people's identities. This contributes to a more welcoming and inclusive environment.
</div>
  </div>

<Heading
  name="Dos and Don'ts
"
  level="h2"
  style="h2"
  type="article"
/>{' '}

<DoAndDont
  direction="horizontal"
  doChildren={<p>Use "Save" for a button that commits changes.</p>}
  dontChildren={
    <p>
      Use "Execute" for the same purpose, as it may have a violent connotation.
    </p>
  }
/>

{' '}

<DoAndDont
  direction="horizontal"
  doChildren={
    <p>
      Use gender-neutral language like "they/them" when the gender of the person
      is not known or relevant.
    </p>
  }
  dontChildren={<p>Default to using "he/him" as a generic pronoun.</p>}
/>

{' '}

<DoAndDont
  direction="horizontal"
  doChildren={<p>Label a button "Submit Feedback."</p>}
  dontChildren={
    <p>
      Label it "Whistleblow," as it might have a negative or confusing
      implication.
    </p>
  }
/>

{' '}

<Heading
  name="Writing Alt Text
"
  level="h2"
  style="h2"
  type="article"
/>
Describe the function or content of an image without stating the obvious. Alt text
should provide equal access to the image's information or function. Limit the use
of phrases like "image of" or "graphic of" unless necessary for understanding the
image.
<DoAndDont
  direction="horizontal"
  doChildren={<p>Company logo"</p>}
  dontChildren={<p>Image of a company logo</p>}
/>

{' '}

<Heading
  name="Complex Content
"
  level="h2"
  style="h2"
  type="article"
/>
For diagrams, infographics, or complex images, provide a textual alternative nearby
or a link to a detailed description.

{' '}

<Heading
  name="Videos and Multimedia
"
  level="h3"
  style="h3"
  type="article"
/>
Offer subtitles and a transcript for audio or video content. Use descriptive captions
for any text displayed within the video.

{' '}

<Heading
  name="Validation Messages
"
  level="h3"
  style="h3"
  type="article"
/>
Use language that provides constructive feedback without blaming the user.
<DoAndDont
  direction="horizontal"
  doChildren={<p>Please enter a valid email address.</p>}
  dontChildren={<p>Invalid input! You failed to enter a correct email.</p>}
/>

{' '}

<Heading
  name="Conclusion
"
  level="h2"
  style="h2"
  type="article"
/>
Inclusive language in a app is not a one-time task but an ongoing commitment. It's
crucial to keep updating and reviewing the language to ensure it remains as inclusive
as possible. This requires not just initial implementation but also regular testing
and revisions. Always refer to established guidelines such as WCAG 2.1 for the most
current best practices.
